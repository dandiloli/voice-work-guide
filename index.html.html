Voice Work Guide

Everything you need to start earning with your voice — narration, singing, and faith-based opportunities.

Record books aloud from home and get paid per finished hour or through royalties. Flexible, remote, and beginner-friendly.

—
You record yourself reading books aloud, then deliver finished audio files to a platform or author. Work is almost entirely remote and freelance — you set your own schedule.
—
Narrators are paid either a flat rate per finished hour (PFH) of audio, or through royalty share — earning a cut every time the audiobook sells.
—
The global audiobook market is projected to grow from $14B in 2026 to $93B by 2034. Demand for narrators is high across all genres, especially faith and self-help.
1
Microphone — A USB condenser mic like the Blue Yeti or Audio-Technica AT2020 ($100–$150). No need for anything expensive to start.
2
Quiet space — A closet full of clothes or carpeted room works. Avoid hard walls. Turn Wi-Fi off on nearby devices to prevent hidden static.
3
Recording software — Audacity (free) is enough to start. Learn ACX's audio requirements early: noise floor, file format, and room tone at start/end of each chapter file.
4
Demo reel — Record 2–3 short samples (1–2 min each) in different genres: fiction, nonfiction, and one specialty like Christian/inspirational.
5
Read from a screen — Use a tablet or Kindle set to airplane mode. Avoid paper page-turning sounds that ruin takes.

PFH = per finished hour of audio. A 6-hour audiobook at $100/PFH = $600 total. Royalty share pays monthly based on sales — more risk but passive income potential if the book sells well. ACX bounty program also pays an extra $75 when a new Audible member buys your book as their first title.

Christian nonfiction, devotionals, Bible studies, and inspirational memoir are consistent top sellers on Audible and Apple Books. This is a real niche with steady, consistent demand.
1
On ACX — Filter projects by "Religion & Spirituality." Mention your faith background and familiarity with scripture in your profile bio.
2
Christianbook.com (CBD) — Occasionally lists narrator opportunities for their audio catalog. Worth checking regularly.
3
Major Christian publishers — Focus on the Family, Tyndale, Thomas Nelson, Zondervan all have audio divisions. Cold outreach with a faith-specific demo can land you on their radar.
4
Librivox (librivox.org) — Free volunteer platform. Great for practice with public domain Christian classics (Spurgeon, Tozer, etc.). Builds your reel before paid work.
Include a 60–90 second faith/inspirational demo clip on every platform profile. Authors of Christian books specifically seek narrators who understand the tone and won't stumble over theological terms.
1
Set up your recording space and mic. Test with Audacity. Run a sample through ACX's free audio check tool to confirm it meets their standards.
2
Record 2–3 demo clips (fiction, nonfiction, faith/inspirational). Keep each 1–2 minutes.
3
Create profiles on ACX and Fiverr simultaneously. Upload demos. Write a bio highlighting vocal style, genres, and any relevant background.
4
Start auditioning on ACX. Target short books (under 4 hours) to build your first credits quickly.
5
After 3–5 completed titles, expand to Upwork and Reedsy for higher-paying clients. Add INaudio for wider distribution reach.
6
Build toward publisher-direct relationships once you have strong reviews and a body of work.

Record vocal tracks for artists, producers, and ministries from home. Deliver audio stems and get paid per song or project.

—
Session singers record vocal tracks for other artists, producers, and content creators remotely. You record your parts at home and deliver audio stems/files.
—
Work includes lead vocals, backing/harmony vocals, jingles, commercials, film/TV, video game soundtracks, and worship music.
—
Same home studio setup as narration works here — a decent condenser mic, quiet space, and a basic DAW.
1
Microphone — Blue Yeti or AT2020 USB condenser ($100–$150) to start. Upgrade to XLR later for better quality.
2
DAW — GarageBand (free on Mac), Audacity (free, any OS), or Reaper ($60). Used to record, edit, and export clean vocal stems.
3
Audio interface (optional upgrade) — If using an XLR mic, Focusrite Scarlett Solo (~$120). Improves quality significantly and opens up better mic options.
4
Demo recordings — Record 2–3 short clips across different styles (pop, R&B, gospel/worship). These are your portfolio — quality matters more than quantity.
5
Closed-back headphones — For monitoring while recording. Prevents bleed from playback into your mic.
Beginner / Fiverr
$30–$100
Mid-level session
$100–$300

Rates are typically per song or per project. Some singers charge hourly. Jingle and commercial work pays higher flat rates. Royalty deals are less common in session singing — most work is flat fee paid on delivery.

Worship music is one of the most consistent niches in session singing. Churches, Christian artists, and ministries regularly need session vocalists for recordings, albums, and backing tracks.
1
AirGigs & SoundBetter — Both have active Christian/worship clients. Tag your profile with "worship," "gospel," "CCM," and "Christian" so you surface in those searches.
2
WorshipTeam.com / PraiseCharts — Platforms used by worship leaders and church music directors. Some list session vocalist opportunities and connect you with worship teams needing studio singers.
3
SoundCloud & YouTube — Post worship covers and original demos. Many Christian producers and artists scout talent directly from these platforms.
4
Direct outreach — Contact Christian labels (Integrity Music, Provident Label Group, Centricity Music) or indie worship artists about session work. A short email with a demo link goes a long way.
5
Local churches — Many larger churches produce original worship albums and need backing vocalists. Being plugged in locally leads to studio sessions and referrals.
A worship-specific demo (30–60 sec of a well-known CCM song or original worship piece) on your AirGigs and SoundBetter profiles makes you immediately findable by Christian clients searching for a vocalist who understands the genre.
1
Set up your recording space, mic, and DAW. Record a test vocal and listen back for noise, clarity, and tone.
2
Record 2–3 demo clips across different styles. Include a worship/gospel clip if that's your niche.
3
Sign up on AirGigs first (quickest to launch) and Fiverr simultaneously. Upload demos, write a bio, set competitive entry-level rates to attract first clients.
4
Deliver your first 5–10 projects with fast turnaround and clean audio. Reviews are your currency early on.
5
Once you have reviews, apply to SoundBetter to reach higher-paying clients and larger productions.
6
Build a presence on SoundCloud or YouTube with worship covers and original sessions to attract inbound clients outside the platforms.
